Title: Controlling Upconversion Nanocrystals for Emerging Applications
Author: Bo Zhou, Bingyang Shi, Dayong Jin, Xiaogang Liu
Affiliation: Nature Nanotechnology Review, 2015
Date: 2026-05-15

1. Introduction & Historical Background
- Upconversion: anti-Stokes emission where low-energy photons produce high-energy luminescence
- First conceived by Bloembergen in 1959 (Nobel Prize in Physics 1981)
- Landmark experiment by François Auzel in 1966: Yb3+ sensitized Er3+/Tm3+
- Ovsyankin and Feofilov independently observed Yb3+-sensitized upconversion (1966)
- Early focus: laser and optical tech; later shifted to biomedical applications
- Key challenge: making tunable, biocompatible nanomaterials with high efficiency

2. Fundamental Considerations
- Upconversion nanocrystals: inorganic host + trivalent lanthanide ions (Ln3+)
- 4fn electrons shielded by outer 5s/5p → weak electron-phonon coupling, sharp emission
- Requires low-phonon-energy crystalline host to suppress non-radiative decay
- Sequential (not simultaneous) photon absorption via long-lived intermediate states
- Four mechanisms: ESA, ETU, CSU, and Photon Avalanche
- ETU (energy transfer upconversion) dominates in sensitizer-activator systems
- Dexter's theory: S-A distance and spectral overlap are critical for energy transfer

3. Upconversion Mechanisms & Dynamics
- ESA: single ion absorbs sequential photons to reach emitting level
- ETU: two excited ions interact, one decays, the other is promoted
- CSU: no intermediate states — requires cooperative sensitization
- EM-mediated upconversion: core-shell with energy migration via Gd sublattice
- Rate equation model: population dynamics of 5-level system
- Judd-Ofelt theory determines microscopic parameters
- Quantum yield limit for ideal two-photon upconversion ≈ 50%
- Measured QY for 30 nm NaYF4:Yb,Er is only ~0.1% (surface quenching dominates)

4. Controlling Photon Upconversion
- Luminescence enhancement strategies:
  - Epitaxial core-shell growth to minimize surface quenching
  - Surface plasmon resonance (Au/Ag nanoparticles)
  - Crystal field manipulation (Li+/Sc3+ codoping)
  - Broadband/super-high power excitation
  - Energy clustering at sublattice
  - Sensitization with IR dyes
- Emission colour tuning strategies:
  - Core–shell design with shell thickness control
  - Dopant composition and concentration adjustment
  - Power density and excitation wavelength control
  - Host lattice engineering
  - Energy migration-mediated upconversion (Gd sublattice)

5. Emerging Applications (Part I)
- Single-particle non-blinking luminescence (2009): NaYF4:Yb,Er tracking/imaging
- Rewritable optical storage (2010): NIR-light-responsive ordered nanopatterns
- NIR photocatalysis: TiO2:Er or hybrid plasmonic-semiconductor systems
- NIR-light-responsive transistor: 3D photonic crystals + NaYF4:Yb,Tm + CNT films
- Fibre-optic sensing: remote single-nanoparticle detection (1000× better than QDs)
- Upconversion lasing and waveguide amplification

6. Emerging Applications (Part II)
- Latent fingerprinting and security ink printing with NIR-to-visible conversion
- RGB printing and multicolour barcoding
- Lifetime-domain encoding for anti-counterfeiting and data storage
- Ultrasensitive thermometers / pressure sensors (Er3+ spectral response)
- Magnetic manipulation of upconversion emission (NaGdF4 nanoparticles)
- SPECT in vivo whole-body 3D imaging (153Sm-radioactivated nanoparticles)
- Self-assembled 2D superlattices of fluoride nanoplates
- Full-colour volumetric 3D display using pulse-duration-sensitive nanoparticles

7. Summary & Outlook
- Interface engineering is the central bottleneck for efficiency
- Hybrid core–shell architectures balance multiple constraints
- Multi-modal integration (imaging, therapy, sensing) is key future direction
- Computational methods enable rational design of complex materials
- Translation from lab to commercial products remains a major challenge
- Security, display, and photonics are promising near-term application areas
